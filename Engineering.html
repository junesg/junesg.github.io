Humanitarian Engineering

Engineering that serves humanity

Dartmouth Humanitarian Engineering Rwanda Pico-hydro Project

In the summer of 2013, I went to Banda, Rwanda with a group of 7 amazingly self-disciplined, comapssionate, and sophisticated engineers from Dartmouth. We rebuilt one Pico-hydro electric power site and mended another one. We also looked at the social impact of our hydro-power station. My Rwandan humanitarian engineering experience was one that I will never forget. 

Prior to departure, the team and I were lucky to have received weeks of training from Professor Charles Sullivan. I learnt the principles of the electrical charging system, and practiced on dealing with the system when problems occured. 

I was mainly the "electrical" engineer on the team, You can read my blogpost here. The full story of our travel is here. 

The Banda village we went to had no running water and electricity, and we were very happy that our electrical charging stations have been providing the sole source of energy for local business. Cell phone charging shops and barber shops were able to operate on a daily basis, and households were also able to light up their houses at night using electric lamps. The story of how engineering improves people's life can not be more tangible in Banda.

      Conducting Polymers

      PANI, intrinsic conducting polymers (ICP) polyaniline (PAni), changes structure and color as it goes through electrochemical oxidation.       Where it all begins

      ENGS20 Intro to Engineering (2009)
      Affordable Health Care

      Ah, ENGS20. It is the first class that all engineers will take. In which we come up with a product (often mechanical), evaluate our product, and demonstrate the business value (including analysis of revenue, turnover, and long term profit) of our invention. 

      I was in a team with Alan Salas, Taylor Gray, and Chris Bodine. The problem we tried to tackle is deep vein thrombosis (DVT).  DVT is the formation of a blood clot within a deep vein, and it often occurs on wheelchaired patients. DVT is dangerous when the blood clot formed travels to the patient's vital organs, such as the lung, and causes blockage. Wheelchair users who keep their legs elevated at times can reduce their risks of developing DVT. Modern electrical wheel chairs are equiped with leg-raisers for this reason but they are too expensive and not covered by health insurance

      We tackle this issue by considering an easily adjustable wheelchair raiser, that is also cheap and detachable. 

      The project was fun, and I began to like Engineering! (Prior to taking Engineering classes, I was taking physics honor classes and philosophy).
