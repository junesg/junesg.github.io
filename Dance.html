Choreographies and Performances

Story: I started dancing Chinese dance since I was 6, and I never stopped. As I moved to Singapore and the states, dance became part of my life. I was fortunate to have received great after-school dance lessons in Shenzhen Xingzhi Professional Technical School and Raffles Junior College. I carried this passion to Dartmouth College, and continued the legacy of the Dartmouth Chinese Dance Troupe (2009-2013) as a dancer and a choreographer. When I moved to University of Michigan, I co-founded the Moli Performing Art Ensemble (links  here and here) .  Chinese dance is more than an art form, it is a philosophy. The circles, lines and twists in Chinese dance moves all carry profound cultural interpretation of this world. As Chinese dancers, we emphasize elegance, unity and skills. The dance styles range from ancient, classical dances, to tribal dances (56 tribes in China). Below shows some of the dances I have choreographed, or performed. 

Snow 雪

Original Choreographer: 
Wei Wei (Raffles Junior College'07)
Modified:
Yuan (June) Shangguan (Umich 2014)

This dance celebrates the beauty of michigan winter with snow flakes flowing in the sky. 
In winter, the wind swirls and snow flakes drift down. The snow flakes first flow gently. As the wind gets stronger, snow storms form. This dance mimics the snow scene where each dancer is a single snow flake. Unique yet blending into the scene as part of the unity. Dancers elegantly create an area of their own on the stage.

Arriving Peacock  孔雀飞来
(Tribal, Dai)

Choreographers: 
Cacey Tang'11,  Yuan (June) Shangguan'13 (Dartmouth)
Dancers:
Cacey Tang'11, Yuan (June) Shangguan'13
Si Chen (PhD),  Jie Sun'12, Hillary Cheng'12, Raina Lin'11

Peacock dance originates from the beautiful Yunan province in China, and is the signature dance style of the Dai tribe. Peacock dancers wear elegant long dresses, and step softly as if treading on the village mud roads. Their arms, hands and torso mimic the agile movements of peacocks, which are seen as the most beautiful bird on earth. 
This particular dance is bubbly and also aims to showcase the elegance of the Dai tribe women.

胭脂泪(The Tears of Rouge)

Choreographer: 
Yuan (June) Shangguan'13 (Dartmouth)
Dancers: 
Mulin Xiong'16, Bonita Chen'16, Ellen Weburg'14, Eileen Hu-Wang'14, Yuan (June) Shangguan'13

The tears of rouge is about the story of dancing women in the French Concession of Shanghai in 1930+.  During that period, Chinese culture experienced a huge influx of western influence. French hairstyles, fashion, lifestyles and dance styles are fused with Chinese ones to create a unique breed wormen working in the Shanghainese nightclubs. In this dance, these women work for a better future, but at the same time suffer from loneliness, and the loss of their identity.

奋斗(Striving)
Raffles Junior College 2008 Annual Dance Concert

Choreographer:
Yuan (June) Shangguan (Raffles Junior College'08)
Dancers: 
Raffles Junior College Chinese Dance Troupe

"Striving" is a classical-modern fusion dance. It bespeaks the idea of greatness, sacrifice, and generations that pursue the same ideals. The dancers became a unity through the dance moves. When one sacrifice is made, many others join the unity.  Striving symbolizes how Chinese culture emphasizes community, togetherness and the constant pursuit of betterment of life.
